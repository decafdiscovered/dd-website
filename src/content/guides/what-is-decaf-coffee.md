If you've ever stood in the coffee aisle wondering what "decaf" actually means, you're not alone. Coffee was starting to wreck my sleep and my stomach, and I thought I was going to have to ditch it altogether. Then the algorithms started showing me Craft Decaf, and that's where my journey began.

So here's the plain-English version — no jargon.

## The Simple Answer

Decaf is just regular coffee with most of the caffeine removed. That's it.

It comes from the same coffee beans, grown the same way, roasted the same way. The only difference is an extra step before roasting where the caffeine gets taken out.

During my research I found out that decaf was actually invented by accident in 1903 by a German merchant, Ludwig Roselius, when a shipment of coffee beans got soaked in seawater!!

It's not a different plant. It's not "fake coffee." It's just coffee, minus the buzz.

## How Does the Caffeine Get Removed?

There are a few different methods, and honestly, you don't need to memorise them to enjoy a good cup. But here's the gist:

**Swiss Water Process** — This uses just water and time to gently pull the caffeine out of the beans. No chemicals involved, which is why you'll often see it marketed as a "natural" decaf.

**Carbon Dioxide (CO2) Process** — This uses pressurised CO2 to target the caffeine specifically. It's popular for larger-scale decaf production.

**Sugar Cane / Ethyl Acetate (EA) Process** — This one uses a solvent derived from fermented sugar cane. You'll sometimes see it labelled "naturally decaffeinated" because the solvent comes from a plant source rather than being synthetic. It's a distinct method from the more generic solvent-based decaf, so if you spot "EA" or "Sugar Cane" on a bag, that's what it's referring to.

If this all sounds a bit science-class, don't worry, the method mostly affects things like cost and how the beans are marketed, not whether your cup tastes good. That comes down to the quality of the beans and how they're roasted, same as with regular coffee.

## Is Decaf Really Caffeine-Free?

Not entirely, but close. Decaf coffee usually still has a small amount of caffeine, so it's better to think of it as "caffeine-light" rather than "caffeine-free."

A regular cup might have around 95mg-150mg of caffeine, while a decaf cup usually has somewhere between 2–5mg. So if you're avoiding caffeine for sleep, health, or just personal preference, decaf is a solid choice, but it's not a hard zero.

## Why Does Decaf Sometimes Taste Different?

This is the question I get asked the most. A few honest reasons:

- **Freshness matters more with decaf.** Because it's a smaller market, decaf beans can sometimes sit on shelves longer before you buy them, and coffee loses its best flavour over time.
- **The removal process can affect the bean slightly.** Not in a bad way necessarily, just a different way, some people notice decaf can taste a touch milder or less bright than its caffeinated twin.
- **Not all decaf is created equal.** Just like with regular coffee, there's a big difference between a budget supermarket decaf and a well-roasted speciality one. A poor decaf experience is usually about the specific bag, not decaf coffee as a whole.

The good news: a well-made decaf, brewed properly and drunk fresh, can taste genuinely great — you'll find plenty of examples in my [honest reviews](/reviews). You shouldn't have to compromise just because you're skipping the caffeine.

## Should You Try Decaf?

If any of these sound like you, decaf is worth a go:

- You love the taste and ritual of coffee but want to cut back on caffeine
- You're sensitive to caffeine and it affects your sleep
- You want an evening coffee without staring at the ceiling at 1am
- You're pregnant or have been advised to limit caffeine
- You just fancy trying something different

There's no rule that says coffee has to keep you wired to be worth drinking.

## Final Thought

Decaf coffee isn't a compromise or a lesser version of "real" coffee — it's just coffee with the caffeine dialled down.
